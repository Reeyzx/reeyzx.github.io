# Devlog 2: Crossroad of Tank Tracks

## Recap [Excerpt from Devlog #1]:
> In the previous car chassis, each wheel had one raycast (4 total), while the tank demo has 10 wheels. If I only raycast the wheels, the visual track may clip through the ground on uneven terrain. If I add raycast to the track, how many do I need before the game lags? This will not interfere with the core gameplay, but it is a feature enhancement worth considering.

## What to Achieve?
A straightforward yet complicated task: developing the tank tracks (also commonly known as "caterpillar tracks").

In this session, I backtracked to the previously unresolved problem of tank tracks. Initially, the main concern was performance, as seen in the excerpt. However, upon analyzing the task up close, the actual roadblock quickly shifted to the matter of how to approach the technical implementation.

---

## The Problem

Contemporary 3D tank games are less common compared to other genres like first-person shooters. Therefore, documentation appear less widespread. Based on research, I noticed three categories of tank game approaches:

# #1. Texture Scroll Method

### **What it is:**
- This is most common in 2000s web games, when computing technology was weaker. Since the tank's wheels cannot move independently (to save resources), the track must look like "it is doing something." A simple way is to apply a texture to the track model and move the texture. This way, the player feels it is somewhat realistic.

### **What the problem is:**
- This is the easiest solution by far. However, this approach is fundamentally incompatible with my hull, because the wheels can fold and extend as part of the suspension system. The track, as a result, cannot be a static model.

# #2. Individual Segments

### **What it is:**
- This is the most realistic approach, used in tank simulations. Each segment in a tank track is modeled, and they run along a loop based on mathematical calculations.

---

### **What the problem is:**
- This is the most realistic solution, but also the most resource intensive. According to [a KitMaker post](https://forums.kitmaker.net/t/number-of-track-links-on-some-us-tanks/26658), the US M103 Tank uses approximately 87 track links per side. 174 individual meshes can potentially result in lag spikes for lower‑end players.
- I'm not an avid military enthusiast. This means I lack professional knowledge in exactly how a tank should look. If I happen to model the segment incorrectly, there would be scrutiny from those more knowledgeable.

# #3. Mesh Deformation

### **What it is:**
- A "track mesh" is modeled as one singular piece and then rigged. This is then programmed to run along a loop based on mathematical calculations. The triangle/poly count remains the same as the former, but it effectively reduces a track's item count from 87 to 1. I also discovered [a helpful guide for reference](https://blog.habrador.com/2014/06/how-to-make-tank-tracks-unity-blender.html?m=1). The developer can maximize performance while balancing realism, which is a good middle ground.

### **What the problem is:**
- Modeling and rigging the track itself require time spent in an alternate platform (e.g., Blender), and I am under a tight time constraint to ship the product (low tolerance for failure). The addition of new platforms introduces higher risk.
- As a solo developer, I think flexibility is very crucial. Hypothetically, if a playtester wants to try out a hull with a different wheel configuration, I might be unable to reuse the existing track model. A modular solution may be more preferable.

---

## Weighting The Options & Compromise

Based on the existing solutions, I identified these key concepts:

- **#1:** The solution should aim to convince; perfection comes second. Many players genuinely enjoyed playing web games, and they didn't complain about the unrealistic wheels.
- **#2:** Realism is a double‑edged sword.
- **#3:** Using #2 as a starting point to optimize has merits, but the solution needs to be reusable.

---

## End Product

To summarize, the final tank track system works in the following ways:

- (1) Upon game start, it directly scans for existing wheels in a hull, ensuring modularity.
- (2) After the "shape" is calculated, it generates the tracks using native cuboid parts. This eliminates the need to model any instances beforehand.
- (3) To cut down on excessive instances, the track segment between two wheels is a single part. Multiple parts are only used to wrap around wheels, reducing performance strain.

## Afterthought

What I believe is worth noting is that the finalized tank track simply looks like a "thick rubber band" visually. While not realistic in a physical sense (no grip), it gives me room to orient the game into something other than a rigorous military simulator. The game can effectively be themed as sci‑fi, remote control, magical fantasy, or anything not grounded in reality.
