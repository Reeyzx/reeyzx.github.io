# Introduction 

This assignment serves as my first opportunity to conduct an interview with another person, as the role of an interviewer. Prior to the interview, I had spent a fair share of time mapping out my career orientation as a game developer, and finding a suitable candidate to provide insights that is not only applicable to the theme of the assignment, but also beneficial to my personal growth outside of academic context.

When I sat down with Jackie, I initially expected a conversation oriented towards teaching methods and his take on surviving technological change, as my only viable cue was the draft of my interview questions.

Yet, what I got instead was an engaging tale about how he, as a self‑described “selfish” person, learned that being considerate isn’t just "a nice thing to have", but is in fact a professional obligation. While the preparation and delivery of my final video presentation was limited in time and format, it is nevertheless true that the project is thought-provoking regarding my own values as a future game developer.

In this post, I reflect on how Jackie’s journey shaped my understanding of professionalism, adaptability, and what it means to make things for other people, in adherence to the course topic covered throughout BCM313.

---

# Designing Questions & Generating Responses

Before the interview, I used the multi-step framework provided in class to draft questions that moved across a wide range of topic, from storytelling to sense‑making. Using the surface knowledge I already knew about my interviewee, I asked about his early technical interests, his unexpected path into teaching, and his experiences with three major disruptions (Smartphones, COVID, and AI). This structure allowed me to put high emphasis on conversation comfort and authenticity, while ensuring professional values, managing change, and future expectations (the three main points), were covered.

Overall, Jackie's responses were often surprising. When I asked which technological shift required the most adaptation, he said they weren’t directly comparable, but opted to reframe it with his insights instead of rejecting it. If I were to pick the most striking response throughout the entire interview, it would be him admitting, “I’m a selfish person.” Yet also insisted that being considerate was a professional obligation, not a personality trait. I believe this served as one crucial piece of information that would influence me on how I connect his view to the course topics.

# Course Concepts in Jackie’s Story

Jackie’s distinction between personal character and professional performance reflects Erving Goffman’s concept on dramaturgy. Goffman argued that we manage impressions depending on our audience (Goffman, 1959). Jackie consciously performed the role of a considerate educator because his students’ learning is depended on it. 

His identity was also shaped through other people's reactions, which I briefly covered in the presentation. For example, he participated in workshops (which involved communication between tutors and fellow participants), as well as student feedback from the user‑experience classes that he taught. This serves as a clear example of Charles Cooley’s “looking‑glass self”. Drawing from above, we can learn that his professional values were not an innate matter, but constructed through interaction, aligning with social construction. 

Jackie’s self‑description as “selfish” also connects to personality type concepts. Today, we have plenty of tools like the Johari Window and MBTI to help us recognise our behavioral patterns, and we oftentimes treat them in high regard (e.g. "I am an ISTP, so I can't be empathetic"). However, this also fuel concerns that our personalities make us less capable in achieving something. Comfortingly, Jackie showed that personality is not destiny, and we can learn to act in ways that serve others as long as we have the will and motivation to do so.

When the interview shifted to discuss how he navigated disruption, Jackie’s pragmatism perhaps reflects a good mindset required in the Fourth Industrial Revolution, where Klaus Schwab (2016) emphasized the velocity and scope of change. Jackie holds a  flexible stance throughout the discussion, believing we should look for the "good" in every new change, instead of being fixated on the negatives. For instance, he mentioned COVID being an effective acceleration fuel to force a trial on mature but underused technology (online conference). This demonstrates high adaptability, a skill universally applicable in the present day.

We may connect this to the topic of reflective nostalgia. Rather than wistfully longing for a pre‑pandemic past, fantasizing "how the 2010s were all bliss", Jackie reframed these previous times of struggle as good time to gain new flexibility. As the Week 6 handout explains, reflective nostalgia “acknowledges your loss but also the impossibility of ever recovering that former time… It liberates you to find new ways to gain something you still need in very different circumstances.” The famous question of whether the glass is half full or half empty, is a good idiom to reflect on this problem, and Jackie chose to believe it was half full.

# My Values in Response

As a person with a handful of experience mingling in the game development circle, and a creative media education background, I had always thought empathy (and to an extent, creativity) as something you either have or you don’t, in a black-and-white manner. While Jackie agreed that some people can’t be a game developer because they lack certain trait, he didn’t state that the trait must be a natural talent. In fact, it can very well be an acquired skill, as his own story suggested. Being considerate can be a performed obligation rather than an innate trait. The nuance is simply whether you can learn to do so, when you realizes you have a lack thereof.

This interview partially reinforced my belief that some people cannot fulfill a specific role because they lack the necessary qualities. To illustrate this idea, teaching a novice developer to use AI in programming may be futile if they express unwillingness to enrich their communication skills. However, the conversation also taught me that it is unfair to dismiss someone outright before understanding their situation. For example, assuming someone is “generally less capable” because they have poorer language proficiency overlooks their potential entirely.

I asked Jackie how he knows when a specific student has a certain need. His blunt response was that “we just have to try and see what happens.” There’s no formula involved. You simply engage, observe, and then adjust. If talking about how my values developed through this process, perhaps I should be more forgiving towards both to myself and to others, because I learned from here that the determination to enrich oneself is more noble than simply being in a good starting position. This is because the future shifts rapidly, a professional of today can become a learner in tomorrow, which is also applicable vice versa.

# The Presentation Experience

Creating the video presentation required me to distill, condense, and filter Jackie’s 40-minutes long conversation into a clear narrative. I structured it around the three required points, selecting appropriate audio clips that captured his voice as I prepare my script along the way. I believe it can also be said that being concise is a form of consideration in itself, because you have to respect the audience’s time while delivering something meaningful. The end result was a 18-minute long video guided by large texts to emphasize the takeaway from long speeches. 

Editing also gave me space to reflect. When I decide whether to cut a sentence away from the recording, or rearrange a section from a later part of the interview to the earlier part of a presentation, I had to iterate with my intention and ask what the audience would focus on, if differently or not. Ethics is important, and I am aware I shouldn’t misrepresent my interviewee in any way: Is this still true to what Jackie meant? The responsibility to be faithful to someone else’s story is just yet another form of consideration in practice.
