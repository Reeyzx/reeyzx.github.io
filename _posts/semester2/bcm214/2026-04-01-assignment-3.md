# Contextual Statement

## About

My digital artefact is a 1-minute video in the format of a YouTube skippable advertisement. The central argument of this video is to inform the audience that existing “lost item” technologies, such as Apple AirTag, are fundamentally reactive. This means they only locate items after they have been misplaced, but do nothing to prevent the moment of forgetting. 

Based on the actual functionalities of SticknScan (RFID‑based doorframe scanner that alerts users when they leave without the sticker-marked essential items), my video dramatizes this problem via two parallel scenarios. 

Firstly, the first half shows a protagonist overconfidently leaving his apartment, only to discover an hour later (via his phone’s find‑my‑item feature) that his laptop is still at home. The technology works perfectly. Unfortunately, the mistake has already occurred, and the protagonist's audiences were none other than enraged.

Secondly, a time‑reversed scene then demonstrates SticknScan’s preventative logic: As the protagonist opens the door, an embarrassing song blasts, neighbours laugh, and the error is caught on the spot before he ever departs in the first place. 

This contradiction identifies the core limitation of reactive trackers, as well as to showcase SticknScan as a creative innovation to address a blind spot.

## Technical Discussion

Skippable ads typically compress content all the way into the first five seconds to retain viewers, essentially jumping into the climax from the get go. However, I decided to open with a black screen and text (“at night, anywhere city, person with mission”) instead. This "reverse psychology" tactic (withholding action to demand attention) aligns with broader research on strategic silence in communication, where intentional pauses are used to heighten audience focus (Le et al., 2019).

Frankly, time and skill constraints were also factored in to guide the final production result. With insufficient time to gather actors or develop animations, I fixed the camera at the same spot throughout the entire narrative, and used shadows to imply movement (door opening, the protagonist leaving). This technique drew references from early Pixar's work like Toy Story 1, where limited technical capabilities influenced the studio to use legs, shadows, and environmental cues to suggest character action without crossing into the boundary of uncanny valley.

The decision to produce a single 60‑second video was a reflection of my prior experience with longer narratives, even though other compositions like four 15‑second clips (e.g. TikTok, YouTube Shorts) were listed as possibility, and also actively discussed in class. A 15‑second format, while inviting novelty, potentially demands high‑precision animation or drawing skills to establish context quickly. I believe there's a risk where any misstep could result in my work appearing disjointed. By working within a 60‑second structure, I gain a bigger creative freedom maintain narrative coherence using implied actions (shadows, sound) and a clear cause‑and‑effect arc while still respecting the brevity required for an advertisement.


## Technical Grounding

In terms of technical relevance, the artefact engages directly with the subject themes of IoT (Internet of Things), as SticknScan originally did. Regarding IoT, RFID technology already exists in retail security with a purpose to detect thieves carrying unpaid goods, and SticknScan repurposes this existing technology for domestic use, turning the concept of a “tracker” from a passive locator into an active checkpoint. Overall, I believe the product's aim for prevention rather than post‑hoc recovery justifies as an act of social innovation. 

On a final note, I would like to frame this video as a use case visualization of the originally abstract concept from a group pitch. After we learned how the product was supposed to work, the remaining piece in the puzzle was to create a tangible, human‑centred story where the audience can self-insert themselves as the victim who forgot and faced consequences.
