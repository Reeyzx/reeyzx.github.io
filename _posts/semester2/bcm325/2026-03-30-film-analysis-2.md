# Introduction

As the second film analysis, we will go from the cyberpunk alleyways of 2029, to the pod farms of 2199, and finally into the VR slums of 2045. When viewed together, **Ghost in the Shell**, **The Matrix**, and **Ready Player One** each offer a unique take of what happens when humanity intertwine with its own machines. 

In a common perspective, the three traces an evolving anxiety of how we, as humans, will recognize ourselves when the boundaries of the "self" become fluid. The central question lies: What remains of "human", when the body is augmented, simulated, or abandoned?

# Ghost in the Shell (1995): The Cyborg’s Quiet Doubt

Mamoru Oshii's Ghost in the Shell imagines a future where cybernetic bodies are so common that not a single person in society bothers to ask who is human and who is machine. In one quiet elevator scene, Major Kusanagi voices her anxiety about her own humanity, only to be told that such distinctions no longer matter. In this simple statement, it's revealed that there was no discrimination. The only problem was the individual’s self confidence. 

![The Elevator Scene](/assets/images/bcm325_assets/Film/ghost-in-the-shell-elevator.jpg)

This moment captures the film’s deeper concern: Instead of social rejection of cyborgs, the troubling obstacle present itself as the internal fragmentation of identity when the "self" is technologically augmented.

The film portrays cyborg in a way that dismantles traditionally assumed binaries. For example, there is no human/machine, it is simply understood as an individual, a citizen in their own rights. There is no life/death, a cyborg and AI can merge and create a new lifeform. However, the film's focus isn't about celebrating the dynamic, but moreso focused on making those slotting categories irrelevant. 

Kusanagi’s body is a shell, while her ghost is data. From the pure perspective of appearance, there is minimal difference between a cyborg and a human. Therefore, the world around her functions pragmatically, not ideologically. We can see filler characters as ordinary commuters and pedestrians, and treat technology as an extension of self, rather than a threat. This aligns with the “pragmatist” stance where AI and augmentation simply serve as a tool to boost human capacity, without erasing the individual’s place in society.

On the other hand, we have the Puppet Master, who is a sentient AI, playing the role of an technical optimist. The Puppet Master had a vision to merge up with Kusanagi to create something superior, beyond human limitation. Yet, the film turned what's supposed to be an act of "holy transcendence" into a mundane rebirth. 

![Ending Scene](/assets/images/bcm325_assets/Film/the-merge-is-evolution-past-humanity-1633139322.jpg)
> Believe it or not, nothing much happens in the ending

After fusion, this new entity still exists in a gritty bureaucratic world. The ending presents a world where technology has simply become mundane. The tension lies not in machine oppression, but in the quiet, persistent question: if the self can be infinitely modified and rewritten, what remains uniquely me? Oshii leaves that question open, suggesting that in a pragmatist future, the cyborg’s greatest struggle is not with society, but with its own reflection.

# The Matrix (1999): The Body as Fertilizer

Wachowskis' The Matrix took a radically dark turn to present a late-stage cyberpunk anxiety, where the body is not just a prison but used as a resource. In this universe, humans are grown in pods with their energy harvested by machines. Yet, while gruesome to watch, this setup mirrors an uncomfortable reality. In real life, our bodies also decompose and nourish the earth, so we can also argue our bodies are being harvested in some way (eventually). The only difference is who present as the benefactor. In The Matrix, the machines have simply replaced nature as the ultimate consumer.

I believe this metaphor makes the film’s moral clarity more complicated. Throughout the plot, we can see Neo and Morpheus enter a neverending struggle to reclaim “the realness”, but what makes reality superior? 

![Steak](/assets/images/bcm325_assets/Film/steak.jpg)

Cypher’s steak scene serves as a good challenge: The steak is code (fake), but the taste and pleasure are real to the person who eats. Later on, Cypher chooses comfortable illusion over hard truth, and the film punishes him for it, which is a classic "you reap what you sow" consequence.

Yet, the sequels reveal that Zion’s fight is cyclical, one might even claim it's futile effort. When we look beyond the first film, the main cast's triumphant ending begin to feel less like a path to victory, and more like an edgy small time rebellion that might never change the underlying structure.

![Zion](/assets/images/bcm325_assets/Film/zion.jpg)
> City of Zion. Is a life in the wasteland worth it?

Perhaps the real message is not about salvaging humanity from simulation. Instead, we should reflect about preventing such a doomsday scenario from happening at all. The Matrix works better as a cautionary tale, illustrating what happens when we cede control of reality to systems whose motivation we no longer understand. Technically speaking, the boundary between what is organic and what is artificial becomes irrelevant when both are reduced to fuel for disposal. In that sense, the film’s enduring question isn’t “red pill or blue pill,” but how we let the choice become necessary in the first place.

# Ready Player One (2018): Escape Reveals Systematic Failure

Steven Spielberg's Ready Player One presents cyberspace as a “consensual hallucination” in the most literal way possible. The OASIS is a fully immersive VR universe where millions escape a decaying 2045. Personally, as a developer on Roblox (whose directors used to masquerade the platform as metaverse), I recognize this parallel ambition: Halliday likely originally intended to craft a world to inspire wonder. Tragically, the plot reveals how escapism take over and dominates when reality offers no alternative towards life satisfaction. 

![Real life of Ready Player One](/assets/images/bcm325_assets/Film/ready_player_one_still.png)

We can observe that players no longer use the OASIS to pass an afternoon. Instead, they quite literally live in it, making little difference compared to opioid abuse.

However, I hesitate to call the OASIS a trap, because trap implies the creator intended to cause harm and profit from it. Rather, the OASIS became a refuge simply because society outside collapsed, and as the final scene reveals, Halliday was one victim himself. 

The real problem isn’t IOI’s greed, but a grim reality that the world got so broken that entertainment became a tool of mental survival. Halliday admits he was afraid of real life and hid inside his creations. What came after was that he passed his regret to Wade, who wins control but chooses to shut the OASIS two days a week, forcing people to “touch grass.”

> Off screen, billions struggle in the wasteland as the triumphant hero engage in intimate contact.

Alas, even though the ending was supposed to be positive, I believe the the solution feels personal, not structural. If we force the drug addicts outside the drug den, what will they do instead? The film celebrates teamwork and nostalgia, but it doesn’t ask why the real world failed. As an aspiring developer, that omission hurts because we tend to be the first line of stakeholders to be accused of "indoctrinating the innocent with malice" in a cyberspace. Reflecting on the self, cyberspace, be it sophisticated Virtual Reality or simple web games, should be purposed as opt-in entertainment to enrich an afternoon, not a crutch for a broken society. 

Ready Player One works as a teenage “save the world” fantasy with clear good and bad guys. But beneath the spectacle, a shallow critique exposes a problem where addiction to the virtual is treated as a matter of individual will, not the systemic decay that made the OASIS the only livable space left. I acknowledge Halliday’s guilt, but it was also tragic that the film never fully confronts the bad actor who enabled a generation to opt out of reality.

# Conclusion

Taken together, these three films show a shift in focus. Ghost in the Shell worries about what we become when we merge with machines, The Matrix fears that reality itself might be a lie, and Ready Player One accepts this very lie as a symptom of a society that we failed to fix. Each of the films offers a different answer, but the underlying question remains urgent. As we build more immersive technologies, will we be able to utilize them to extend ourselves for the greater good, or to escape ourselves and shove ourselves into a compromised situation for "the other individuals" to exploit? It is a good reminder that the future isn't purely about what technology we can come up with, but how we should strategize ourselves, when the time finally comes and we have to live in it.
