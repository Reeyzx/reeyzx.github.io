# From Speed to Span to Value: High‑Speed Rail in 2050

We previously left off in year 2030, where we discussed the future opportunities and challenges of Automated Vehicles under Wendell Bell’s 3-part future framework: Possible, Probable, and Preferable. Now, our timeline shifts to 2050, which is essentially two decades after. To bridge the two discussions relevantly, I will focus on the topic of high‑speed railway, sharing a common theme on logistics and transportation.

High‑speed rail has been a mature invention for long, starting as early as the 1960s in Japan as Tokaido Shinkansen. Today, it is not difficult for one to hitch a ride if you happen to be in Asia‑Pacific or Western Europe. For such a “normalized” technology, how does the distant future present itself?

---

# Possible: Raw Speed

In possible futures, Bell proposes that the constraint is feasibility, not practicality. We can argue that speed is the easiest dimension to exaggerate, because it’s simply one single measurable number that captures our raw imagination. The fantasizing of speed is quite common among us, that even children may sketch rocket‑train hybrid vehicles.

These creative imaginations involved no pain on land acquisition, regulations, or immense funding. In this way of thinking, “possible” rewards conceptual leaps, and raw speed is the most vivid leap.

## Examples

We can see this in professional and academic circles. Based on my prior research, I noticed three prominent attempts at making “hyper high‑speed rail”:

- Tweaking existing high‑speed rail to break limitations (Japan’s ALFA‑X Shinkansen, pushing conventional steel‑wheel technology toward 400 km/h and beyond). This is optimization focused, and is considered the traditional stream.
- Via “alternate” methods, like experimenting with maglev technology (China’s 600 km/h CRRC maglev, and Japan’s Chuo Shinkansen maglev slated for 505 km/h operation).
- The long‑debated hyperloop (projects like Hardt Hyperloop in Europe and Virgin Hyperloop’s legacy), as well as other unconventional transportation methods that sparks debate whether it should be considered “railway”.

![Conventional Unit, Maglev, and Hyperloop](/assets/images/bcm325_assets/2050/3-papers-trains.png)

In the coming decades, we will see a lot of prototypes announced by government sources and tech giants, all trying to surpass each other in terms of who makes the train go faster. In two decades’ time, many of these will make it off the drafting board and be put to implementation. This is an expected result when “everyone throws something to the wall and see what sticks,” combined with the reality of a sheer number of participants.

## Rivalry Against Aviation

Therefore, by 2050, we can picture trains going at a speed of 700 km/h, directly pitting against regional airlines. For reference, an Airbus A320’s cruising speed is roughly 840 km/h (Airbus, 2025). Using aviation as a parallel study, the freedom from customs checkpoint, waiting at the gate, and fear of flying is a fundamental problem that cannot be realistically solved. What would happen next is that high‑speed rail becomes “the peasants’ choice” whenever available, further ingraining this technology in our daily life.

## The Contradiction 

As a hypothesis, a possible future might actually be boring for a passenger in 2050, compared to how we fantasise about a train going “boom” very quickly today in 2026. This is fundamentally different from the previous topic of Empathetic Automated Vehicles, where technology engaged with human emotion. This is because raw speed alone compresses time without necessarily enriching the experience.

---

# Probable: Network Expansion

In probable futures, the constraint becomes institutional reality. Most governments, agencies, and investors don’t fund revolutions, unless said project presents immense previously undiscovered opportunities. Admittedly, this can be considered borderline impossible for high-speed rail, a technology that would have existed close to one century by 2050.

The more realistic outcome however, is the funding of extensions in existing systems. In terms of transit network planning, the span and coverage comes natural. This means more lines, more stations, and more interchanges. Because this is something that we already know how to do, it would be an incremental procress, and it matches how large infrastructure actually grows.

## Diverging Path (Developing v.s. Developed)

Today’s developing countries will enter a construction frenzy at different paces, resulting in a dramatically large span network. Eventually, the centre of attention regarding high‑speed rail would end up in these countries. 

The two cases of China (larger) and Indonesia (smaller) serve as good examples of how high‑speed rail can be done effectively if framed as a national objective to boost the country, instead of as a direct revenue source. 

- China, already operating over 45,000 km of HSR, aims to reach 70,000 km by 2035, connecting all cities above 500,000 people (China State Council, 2021). 
- Indonesia’s Whoosh line between Jakarta and Bandung, built with a business‑to‑business model, has shifted travel behaviour and achieved profitability faster than projected (PT KCIC, 2025).

On the other hand, it is also worth mentioning that many developed countries with an already established high-speed rail system treat railway as a revenue stream. If the system is already not profitable, then further expansion is declared a no go. 

- France’s SNCF Réseau has struggled with TGV profitability, leading to debates about closing unprofitable lines (Autorité de régulation des transports, 2024). 
- Germany’s Deutsche Bahn faces chronic delays due to underinvestment (Allianz pro Schiene, 2025).

![Route map provided by Eurostar](/assets/images/bcm325_assets/2050/eurostar-map-brussels.svg)

This is also a valid, perhaps mundane outcome. When a country is at “maintenance mode” (most land developed, everyone living decent life), there is little incentive to break earth. Scholarly work on infrastructure lock‑in (Mattioli et al., 2020) suggests that once a country reaches a certain level of motorization and aviation saturation, the incremental benefit of new high-speed rail lines declines, making them harder to justify.

## Takeaway

By 2050, the global map of high-speed rail will be starkly divided. We may have vast, high‑density networks in emerging economies, with limited selective upgrades (or unfortunately, stagnation) in older industrialized regions. However, as global citizens, it may be unfair for us to label any side as being inherently superior or inferior, as different countries experiences a different developmental trajectory, and deserves a plan that rightfully suit their needs.

# Preferable: Quality Maintenance

Ideally, the timely needs of the passengers will continue to be addressed (instead of neglected) in 2050. In preferable futures, the constraint becomes felt value. If a railway company hands out a survey to the passengers regarding what should happen next, the respondents would most likely gravitate toward ideas that affect quality of life, like the following:

- “I want comfortable seats!” (personal)
- “Make it so that I can bring my pet on board!” (community)
- “Consider importing this eco-friendly train model from Y country!” (global)

These are felt experiences driven by human criteria. "Preferable" naturally centre on human needs, and an average passenger likely craves at least one of the following: Pleasant travel, inclusiveness, and the environment.

Fortunately, it is noted that European research programs target "improvement of passenger comfort and the attractiveness of rail transport" as a key goal for 2050 (De la Guerra, n.d.). Given the span of 2 decades, it is logically sound and supported by evidences that passenger satisfaction would hold a very major influence in the iterative process of enhancing rail services. While I cannot make a reliable prophecy on the contemporary culture in 2050 to gauge the people's exact values, I believe rail operators would have an up-to-date picture of what values passengers appreciate the most, and act in accordance to maintain public support, as much as they would do today.

## Hidden Stake 

Yet, there is a tension behind this seemingly straightforward goal. If I search on the browser questioning what is "good", naturally, I would receive sources that gravitate toward advocating stakeholder’s benefits, but under the assumption that a high-speed rail is already built. However, we oftentimes neglect what is sacrificed to run the railway in the first place. The rail must be placed in a same piece of land where someone originally used it for farming or living. It is a nuanced topic that cannot be discussed in one post alone, but it is something to be acknowledged, nevertheless.

In Canada’s proposed Toronto - Quebec City high‑speed rail corridor, farmers and rural residents reportedly clashed with the development plans. Eventually, the Ontario Federation of Agriculture called for suspension of the project, citing disruption to farmland and livelihoods.

# Conclusion

Bell's three future lenses revealed that high‑speed rail technology by the time of 2050 will not follow a single trajectory. Instead, it will be context-dependent in regional politics. 

The **possible future**, driven by raw speed, grabs our innate imagination but risks becoming mundane once the technology matures. The **probable future**, shaped by institutional reality, reveals a diverging point, in which developing nations builds vast networks as national ambition, while developed countries face stagnation under maintenance‑mode economy. The **preferable future**, centred on human values, demands continuous iteration in improving comfort and inclusivity, but it also forces us to think beyond the values of the riders, which is unfortunately often assumed away when thinking from the perspective of a modern consumer. 

“Proposing action brings responsibilities,” as Wendell Bell cautiously reminds. None of these futures is inevitable, but as futurists and for citizens, we need to be mindful that each corner of the world operates under a different logic. Your neighbors from across the continent may have a different imagination, momentum, and values, and it is absolutely important for one to engage with them in an open manner.
