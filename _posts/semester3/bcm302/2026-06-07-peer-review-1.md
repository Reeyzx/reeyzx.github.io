# [Project 1 -- Mindful Learning](https://wwh1498.wixsite.com/bcm-302/post/mindful-learning-reducing-exam-stress-for-kids)

The concept of Mindful Learning addresses a socially severe issue (exam stress) with a clear target audience (primary school students). It is noted that the core utility of this project relies heavily on **user agency**, signalling voluntary choice to engage with healthy content. 

I believe it is crucial to consider a critical edge case, **institional adaption**. While a boost in popularity is attractive on paper, if a school were to make this compulsory (e.g. students must watch this video and write a 100 word report as homework), the context could shift from voluntary relief to forced participation, and that would inversely cause more academic stress. Local example include the English i-Learner, and the Chinese equivalent (智愛中文平台).

To protect this project's unique vision, I recommend explicitly stating the boundary where/how Mindful Learning should be distributed: Is it a strictly parent-led tool? Or is it self-discovered by the schoolchildren's own tablet? A disclaimer could prevent institutional misuse and the compromise of the platform's core value.

I would also like to mention 2 potential hurdles regarding the iteration methodology:
- Relying on YouTube comments for user feedback may be flawed. According to YouTube's "Made for Kids" (COPPA) policies, comments are automatically disabled for content targeting "kids," which include primary schoolers (age <12). 
- If comments are enabled, a child may be unlikely to leave critical feedback if their parent is also watching the video with them, which risks breaking the feedback loop. 

To capture authentic user data, I suggest designing a digital survey (e.g. Google Form) linked in the video/channel's description. This allows parents to fill it out with their child annoymously.

# [Project 2 -- QueueCue](https://fizaraees1530.wixsite.com/queuecue/blank)

The concept and utility of QueueCue aim to solve a very felt & frustrating problem of **crowdedness**. This project addresses the pain point of unpredictable waiting times by offering a portable mobile-based solution.

Regarding methodology, there is a structural gap between the proposed features of QueueCue and the prototyping tool (Canva) used to demonstrate it.

In the pitch, the feedback loop implies user interaction with the product, but Canva is a static graphic tool. In development, It would be beneficial to transition to an interactive UI/UX tool, such as **Figma** or Adobe XD. These tools allows you to build clickable and transitional flows (e.g. pressing a real button in Cafe A [screen 1] to see its statistics [screen 2]), so your team can demonstrate the prototype's functionalities more authentically without ever needing to code backend, a win-win situation.

In the field of crowdedness detection, Google Maps is already a massive structural competitor. However, they estimates a place's crowdedness by processing anonymized GPS pings from mobile phones, which is inherently unreliable despite offering an enormous global coverage. On the other hand, QueueCue can turn its coverage limitation into a strategic advantage by focusing on **locations where crowdiness don’t just cause discomfort but can completely derail an experience.** In those cases, precision matters a lot, and users need to know exactly how many people are queuing, rather than just an estimate. In light of this, I recommend narrowing the scope to a closed ecosystem (say, a campus hotspot or a very popular café chain).
