# Designing the Absurd: World-Building and the Infinite Loop in *Tower of Literal Randomness*

![The game of TOLR](/assets/images/bcm300_assets/tolr/tower-scenery.png)

### Genre and the "Obby" Cultural Context
In the Roblox ecosystem, the [**"Obby"**](https://roblox.fandom.com/wiki/Obby) (short for obstacle course) is a decade-old, culturally entrenched subgenre of 3D platforming experiences. My game, [*Tower of Literal Randomness*](https://www.roblox.com/games/80809896342264/Tower-of-Literal-Randomness), operates within the "Tower Obby" niche. However, rather than a static sequence of jumps, we are designing an experimental extreme sports event. It captures the lighthearted, viral energy of a "GoPro 4K parkour video", which transforms a simple climbing simulator into a televised spectacle of human perseverance against procedural chaos.

### Theme vs. Setting: Crafting the Spectacle
Following the framework established in Chapter 3, we separated the game's identity into **theme** and **setting**:
- What: The theme is the spectacle and absurdity of an experimental extreme sports event, where "Red Bull-style" athletes conquer a fundamentally chaotic structure. 
- Where: The setting is a Manhattan-inspired grid city that provides the physical environment for this spectacle.

### Setting and World-Building: Synthesizing Urban Philosophy with Procedural Chaos

Image: An unfiltered aerial view of the game's map after research
![God view of TOLR's map](/assets/images/bcm300_assets/tolr/god-view.png)

As emphasized in Chapter 4, effective world-building requires "using real-world inspiration", while maintaining "consistency is key." In my original concept, the pre-prototype featured a soulless, 200x200x1000-stud rectangular tower floating in a void. Because the tower's boundary is fundamentally rigid and cuboid, the surrounding world needed to bend to justify its existence and manage the player's psychological experience. Hence, I looked to real-world urban design to solve two distinct problems: **spatial grounding** and **player retention**.

**The Spatial Anchor: Eixample and Structuralism**

Image: Eixample Grid. Barcelona, Spain
![Eixample Grid](/assets/images/bcm300_assets/tolr/eixample.png)

To ground the tower, we needed an urban grid that could accommodate its massive footprint. We adopted the logic of Barcelona's *Eixample*, originally designed by Ildefons Cerdà. The *Eixample* is defined by its strict, highly rational geometric grid and standardized block sizes (each measuring 113.3 meters), which provide a highly predictable, ordered baseline (Busquets, 2014). This absolute urban order creates a stark, deliberate contrast with the "literal randomness" of the tower's procedurally generated bricks, framing the tower as an anomaly within a structured city.

However, applying traditional Eixample architecture created a genre discrepancy; classical buildings clashed with our blocky, Roblox-native brick aesthetic. To achieve visual and conceptual synthesis, as advised in Chapter 6, we pivoted the architectural style to Dutch Structuralism. Emerging in the mid-20th century, this movement emphasized "labyrinthine clarity" and the use of basic, modular geometric forms (like brick and concrete) to create complex social spaces (Frampton, 2007). By applying Dutch Structuralism, the environment's architecture is designed to share the same fundamental "blocky" visual language as the game's core mechanics. 

Image: Centraal Beheer office building. Apeldoorn, Netherlands
![Centraal Beheer office building](/assets/images/bcm300_assets/tolr/structuralism.jpg)

Note: While the current prototype utilizes placeholder geometry to validate the spatial layout and scale, this conceptual pivot ensures that future iterations will aesthetically unify the city and the tower, preventing the setting from distracting from the core gameplay loop.

**The Psychological Buffer: The "Third Space"**

Image: Central Park. New York City, USA
![Central Park](/assets/images/bcm300_assets/tolr/central-park.jpg)

While the grid and the tower provide the mechanical challenge, early spatial testing revealed a **critical flaw in player retention**: the high-stakes, claustrophobic nature of the climb caused immediate player churn. It revealed players needed a decompression zone, otherwise they quit the game as soon as they step outside the game loop.

To solve this, we carved a Central Park-style environment directly into the city grid. Frederick Law Olmsted's original design philosophy for New York City's Central Park was explicitly to create a "Third Space", which is defined as a psychological refuge and "lungs of the city" designed to alleviate the mental fatigue and claustrophobia of high-density urban environments (Rybczynski, 1999). By integrating this tranquil, open-space environment around the tower's base, we provide players with a necessary psychological buffer. They can socialize, reset, and decompress outside the high-stakes vertical arena, which directly mitigates frustration and improves long-term player retention.

### Key Mechanics and the Infinite Core Loop
As categorized in Chapter 5, we structured our design into distinct mechanical pillars: **core mechanics** (navigating the spawning bricks), **progression mechanics** (the XP and shop system), and **social mechanics** (the multiplayer voting and leaderboard).

The core mechanic relies on three brick types: Red (harmful), White (neutral), and Blue (climbable). These bricks physically spawn and despawn after a set duration within the massive 200x200x1000-stud vertical arena, requiring players to dynamically navigate procedural unpredictability. While the pre-prototype featured a static climb, initial playtesting highlighted a need for sustained engagement, as players naturally end their session after a single ascent. To address this and provide purpose to the procedural generation mechanic, we established a meta-game loop. We iterated on the macro-structure to create an "infinite run" arcade loop:

1. **The Intermission:** Players democratically vote on the gamemode (e.g., "Classic" for standard bricks, "Y4K" for thin, tall bricks), altering the spawn algorithms.
2. **The Ascent & Reset:** Players scale the tower. Reaching the 1000-stud summit immediately drops them back to the ground to initiate a second run.
3. **The Round & Leaderboard:** A "round" operates on a set time limit. The final leaderboard tallies the total altitude and number of completed runs achieved within that single round, rewarding endurance over a single lucky climb.
4. **The Shop:** Players spend earned XP on cosmetics or functional skillsets, completing the meta-progression loop.

### The Three-Act Structure of the Round
Because a single climb from 0 to 1000 studs is a consistent, repetitive test of mechanical execution, the true dramatic pacing occurs at the macro-level of the Round:

* **Act 1: The Warm-Up (Setup):** The intermission and voting phase. Players establish their rhythm, learning the specific spawn timings of the chosen gamemode. The tension is low, and the focus is on mechanical calibration.
* **Act 2: The Grind (Conflict):** The mid-round phase. Players are constantly resetting, trying to beat each other's altitude totals. The procedural randomness peaks, and physical/mental fatigue sets in. This act tests adaptability and endurance against the "literal randomness" of the tower.
* **Act 3: The Final Broadcast (Resolution):** The final seconds of the round. The leaderboard cutoffs are on the line. Players make frantic, high-risk pushes to the top to secure their XP payout before the timer hits zero, culminating in the immediate dopamine hit of the results screen.

### Abstraction and Visual Design
Finally, our visual design embraces what Chapter 8 identifies as "high abstraction." By stripping away realistic textures in favor of simple, color-coded bricks (Red, White, Blue), we reduce overwhelming details to focus the player entirely on the core experience of spatial problem-solving and endurance. This abstraction allows players to concentrate on the mechanical challenge without being distracted by unnecessary visual complexity.

### Iteration and the Path Forward
Our iteration process has been twofold. Macroscopically, we evolved the environment from a void to a cohesive, performance-optimized urban park, and transformed the mechanics from a static climb into a replayable, meta-progressive loop based on initial playtesting feedback. Microscopically, the physical friction of the bricks, the exact timing of the respawn algorithms, and the collision geometry of the Dutch Structuralism placeholders are currently undergoing rigorous balancing. While this post outlines the conceptual world-building and the structural core loop, the granular mechanical balancing, specific player feedback metrics, and environmental refinements will be comprehensively detailed in the final Project Dossier.
