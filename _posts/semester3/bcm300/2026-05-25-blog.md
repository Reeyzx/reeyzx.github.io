### The Illusion of the Peaceful Settler: An Autoethnographic Analysis of Resource Denial in *Catan*

![Typical setup of Catan, preview.](/assets/images/bcm300_assets/colonist/catan.png)


# Context and Industry Origins
First published in 1995, *The Settlers of Catan* is widely recognized as the catalyst for the modern "Eurogame" revolution (Woods, 2012). Designed by Klaus Teuber and originally published by Kosmos, it targeted families and casual gamers transitioning from abstract, elimination-heavy games. While later physical editions featured illustrators like Michael Menzel, it is crucial to acknowledge Teuber’s foundational tile design and aesthetic, which established the visual language that all subsequent artists and digital adaptations have improvised upon. 

My engagement with the game did not begin with physical cardboard, but through *Colonist*, a digital adaptation developed by the independent team behind Colonist.io (first released in 2020). Unlike official licensed versions, *Colonist* operates as an unofficial clone, which adds an extra layer of impersonality: the game enforces its rules without any human negotiation. The original *Catan* has sold more than 40 million copies worldwide (Catan GmbH, 2024). Yet, its many digital clones exist in a legal grey area. This tension between a globally successful brand and unlicensed digital implementations shaped my expectation of the game as a rigid, almost cold system. I entered this experience expecting a standard, popular European tabletop game focused on casual social engagement and friendly competition, but what I found was something far more ruthless, subjectively.

# Acquisition and the Friction of Learning
The digital tutorial was unhelpful; I struggle with memorizing abstract recipes (e.g., "X+Y+Z for a development card"). Instead, I learned collaboratively in a live voice chat with friends. This resulted in a collective, clueless blunder: we placed all our starting settlements on tiles with zero brick access, handicapping ourselves from the outset. 

![An screenshot of a "failed game" where all players placed on non-brick tile, derailing the game.](/assets/images/bcm300_assets/colonist/failed-game-example.png)

This messy learning phase also revealed a nuanced design tension regarding player freedom. In *Colonist*, the session owner can configure the victory point threshold to almost any number, even something as high as 16. However, the game does not warn the player that this threshold might be mathematically impossible to reach due to the limited number of board tiles and physical pieces. The system allows this freedom without understanding the threshold of impossibility, making it difficult to set a hard limit. This highlights a friction between sandbox freedom and rigid game constraints.

# Affective Responses and Mechanical Optimization

My core tactic involve deliberately placing/building my early settlements exactly where an opponent intends to build theirs **(2)**, rendering their existing road(s) useless. When I execute this, the other player often gets mad. I accept this anger as a valid, expected mechanical tradeoff. Unless there is abolute necessity, I usually avoid the psychologically tempting "6" and "8" probability tiles, recognizing them as primary targets for the Robber. Instead, I favor the less preferred, "tier two/three" tiles (5 & 9, 4 & 10) to establish a sustainable, low-profile early-game advantage. 

This lived experience perfectly illustrates Salen and Zimmerman’s (2004) definition of a game as an "artificial conflict with a quantifiable outcome." The "no brick" blunder was so frustrating precisely because the math immediately penalized it, proving the game is a rigid artificial conflict, not a casual sandbox. The quantifiable outcome (victory points) dictates all affective responses.

# Theme vs. Mechanics: The Zero-Sum Reality

![The Death of Major Peirson](/assets/images/bcm300_assets/colonist/John_Singleton_Copley_001.jpg)

And yes, I believe this specific game has a "story," or "theme."

This brings us to the relationship between the game’s mechanics and its stated theme. On the surface, *Catan* presents a neutral narrative framework:
- A **Background** (Catan, an empty island, characterized as lush and a realm of opportunity),
- A **Situation** (the arrival of new peers: "Settlers of Catan"),
- A **Goal** (to become the "best" in this realm).

The setting is simply a convenient grounding mechanism to focus player attention. Simply put, **there are no inherent "bad guys," and this is a "happy place" to live in.**

Hence, the mechanical reality reveals a stark contrast. Sourcing goods, laying roads, and building houses is inherently non-zero-sum; it is a net positive. The controversy arises entirely from the game’s zero-sum win condition. Because there is only one winner, utilitarian value is dictated solely by victory points. **A blocked road loses all utilitarian value** because it no longer contributes to the quantifiable outcome. In real life, you can look at a side street and appreciate its curated aesthetics; in *Catan*, a blocked street is a total loss.

This mechanical ruthlessness mirrors a dumbed-down simulation of 18th-century colonial powers extracting resources and sabotaging each other in a "New World" (the game board), all while maintaining a polite, "noble" facade back in continential Europe (your living room). Institutional narratives often claim games like this "develop social skills." In reality, the most valuable lesson *Catan* teaches is damage control and temper regulation: Essentially, the ability to pretend to be humble while actively slaughtering your friend, and the discipline not to melt down when you are losing.

# The Digital Arbiter vs. Physical Messiness

![Typical aftermath of a table flip incident, Monopoly](/assets/images/bcm300_assets/colonist/table-flip.jpg)

Ultimately, this analysis explains my preference for the digital *Colonist* over the physical board game. In a physical setting, the game relies on a fragile social contract and mutual rule enforcement. The prospect of playing with humans introduces variables the mechanics cannot control. For instance, a half-drunk player misinterpreting a "9" roll as a "10," or a sneaky child quietly collecting resources they are not owed. In the digital version, the code acts as an infallible gamemaster: It guarantees that the ruthless, zero-sum mechanics are enforced without physical loopholes. *Catan* is a brilliant, cutthroat system, and it is best experienced when the system itself is the only authority.
