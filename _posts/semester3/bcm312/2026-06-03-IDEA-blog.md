# The Chicken Who Eats Itself: How Buldak's Hochi Mascot Reframe Animal-Flavored Product Consumption

---

# Background

If you step into a the noodles aisle of a supermarket, you'll see this little guy: A cartoon chicken gasping while spitting fire, eyes wide in tears, sometimes sitting on cheese.

Who's that? This is Hochi, the famed mascot of [**Samyang Buldak**](https://buldak.com/us/) since 2014. You may have laughed at the absurdity of Hochi's comical suffering. Say, a chicken eating chicken noodles, or the fact that you'll also become a parody of him after you boiled the noodles at late night. 

Yet, your laughter isn't coincidental. This is actually the result of a curated design choice that subtly reframes what exactly we're about to eat, effectively turning potential ethical friction of animal product consumption into brand comedy, before we even put the noodles in the shopping cart.

![Image of a box of noodles](/assets/images/bcm312_assets/idea-blog/buldak.png.png)

# Research Question

Thus, my study asks: 

**How does the "suffering-but-funny" Hochi mascot on Samyang Buldak Hot Chicken Ramen packaging use visual rhetoric to frame chicken flavored product consumption as humorous rather than signaling animal abuse, and what does this suggest about how the packaging design of fast-moving consumer goods (FMCG) can use anthropomorphic humor to reframe underlying ethical tension as brand comedy?**

To keep a measurable scope, the research would first start with three popular Buldak variants. The focus scope is on packaging illustration to understand how design silently guides emotional response before our conscious thinking kicks in.

# Significance

One may intuitive think packaging is primarily for decorative purposes, but more crucially we could also say this is the first silent touchpoint between the consumer and the product. 

While domestic Buldak contains real chicken and exports use artificial flavoring, the mechanism to absorb tension around animal associations is representational, regardless of ingredients. With this in mind, as its namesake suggests, Buldak (“fire chicken”) is chicken noodles, hence there's a fundamental limitation where the marketers risk inviting scrutiny if it loudly claims the product is animal/vegan-friendly. While it's entirely appropriate to just not do anything “odd” to steal the spotlight (think about a bland depiction of noodles in a bowl), Hochi’s presence double down on the chicken motif to redirect your attention away from a behind-the-scenes biological reality, towards a very personally felt experience. 

For instance, aggressive sweating signals the product's spice intensity, not a chicken's slaughter distress. Alternately, a smug face signals brand confidence, not a farm animal's contentment. 
By staging the absurdity upfront (the mascot eating the product, merging with ingredients, enduring the heat), the design becomes an effective conversation starter (“look at how weird he is!”). It redirects attention away from invisible animal suffering towards shared endurance (of spice) and brand loyalty instead.

## So what?

Investigating this mechanism matters because the above example quietly trains consumers to process meat with joy instead of friction (even speaking as a meat enjoyer myself). It shows that commercial media is more capable of curating around a tension rather than resolving it, which is a strategy that bridges across grocery categories, not just instant noodles. 

## For whom, does this matter?

This matters for, but is not limited to three groups: 
- Brand strategists may see how humor reduces purchase hesitation.
- Animal advocates learn an engineered duality: Hochi is hyper visible as a mascot and invisible as a food source. 
- Media literate consumers could gain agency by recognizing how design shapes emotion before conscious purchase.

# Preliminary Reading

This section entails how existing researches touch upon these strategies, and where the literature leaves a gap to step into.

### **“If there's an elephant in the room, you won’t worry about the mice.”**

The question of ingredient transparency in mass market instant food is rarely resolved through explicit mentioning. Naturally, consumers encounter the package before they encounter the ingredients list. This is when visual design supplement can actually shine. My hypothesis is that Samyang and the others don't attempt to solve ingredient questions by bold disclosure. Instead, they simply bypass them entirely through what can be coined as "aesthetic framing". Spence and Van Doorn (2022) reveals that packaging elements like curvature, color saturation, and character posture gives people expectations long before the product is consumed. Their findings confirms the idea that a well-designed mascot effectively behave like an “expectation anchor”, redirecting attention from ingredient origins to immediate sansational promises. 

### **"The chicken is a curated chicken, you don't treat him like livestock"**

Metaphorically, the decision to double down on the chicken motif feels rationally counterintuitive. It’s like burying a firecracker underground and sticking a sign on the dirt that says “fireworks”. Why draw attention to the animal origin at all? 

The answer lies in how humour manages tension. **Widolf (2025) finds that meat advertising often uses jokes and visual absurdity as moral disengagement tools.** By making the mascot the source of the joke, the design turns potential ethical discomfort into a shared brand experience. **Bastian and colleagues (2024) add that consumers routinely downplay an animal’s mental capacity to reduce guilt.** Hochi, as instance, accelerates this process by being presented as openly foolish (e.g. exaggerated expression, odd attire choices), so the mental separation happens almost immediately.

Choosing a humanoid chicken over a realistic animal, or a human chef, is also no accident. A realistic animal might remind you of slaughter, while a chef might feel too serious. Anthropomorphism usually shortens psychological distance, and in some cases that **lowers meat purchase intentions among morally concerned consumers** (Gong & Kim, 2024). **Yet, Hochi subverts that pattern,** as we already know his presence is used to pull consumers. 

### The Gap

Existing research on packaging and animal product consumption appears to focus on the ethical side of aspects, such as animal welfare and social justice. Less attention has been paid to how a mascot’s comical suffering might absorb a latent ethical tension without resolving it, a niche that requires a nuanced/interpretative analysis. This study explores that specific visual rhetoric in the context of Asian instant noodle packaging.

# Closing & Next Steps

As a final wrap up, future analysis will continue treating Hochi, an on-the-surface quirky marketing gimmick, as a visual rhetoric case study in friction management. For the next phase, I will finalize three Buldak variants for a comparative visual analysis, and then map out specific design cues for further study.
