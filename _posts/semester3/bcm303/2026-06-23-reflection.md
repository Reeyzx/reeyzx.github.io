## The Comment

One consideration I had to make was cultural barrier, and it shaped how we select the specific social media to promote Qwertz (e.g. RedNote feasibility evaluation). Our project group consists of multilingual members who may know or may not know specific cultural tropes that are tied to languages, and it made me reflect on the identical dynamics back in Qwertz Studio, which I am a part of. The lecture topic of the week is conveniently about memetics: It is best when the team knows what is trendy in a specific region, so they don't make mistake by posting content irrelevant to a cultural setting, be it the Anglosphere, the Sinosphere, or the Iberosphere.

Overall, I am satisfied with the final output.

Something I'm thankful for is that our group did not operate in a "one manager bossing around" hierarchy. The four of us worked in a linear approach, where I laid out what I knew about Qwertz, Riber used that information to formulate a script, Jeff read the script directly as the voice narrator, and Raman composed the presentation video using the voiceover. 

A crucial thing I learned is that we successfully achieved consistency via this method. I've previously experienced pitfalls where a group divides work by 4 like a pie, and everyone works their own portion, and the result doesn't look appealing. This time, there were no clashes because each of us was only doing the specific niche that they excel at.

My decision to not intervene was also informed by an earlier pitfall. In a previous project, we attempted to make Jeff read the same passage again and again as a method to gauge how long the audio file would be, and it disrupted the enthusiasm flow. Therefore, this time I simply laid out what I know, and the rest of us completed their job in their own style. This reinforced my belief that no "master" likes to be lectured at what they perceive to be skilled, especially by someone who does it worse. 

A good team is when everyone is assigned to a desired position, rather than working at full efficiency. Therfore, moving forward, I continue to see coordination as facilitation, rather than management.
